  • Spot the Imposter

    Can you find the impostors on the wall? Among the 420 sea lion skulls above you, we’ve hidden four similarly-sized skulls that belong to other animals. Look closely to spot their differences.

    Touch to learn more about the species hiding among the sea lions.

  • E10

    Sea lion impostor: African lion

    Big cats use their four long canine teeth to grip the neck of prey. A lion crushes an animal’s windpipe to suffocate it. It then uses carnassials—a pair of side teeth that come together like scissors—to shear off pieces of flesh. Meat-eaters don’t chew much, swallowing their food in large chunks.

    • Species

      African lion Panthera leo

    • Collection Info

      Cast skull

  • C17

    Sea lion impostor: gray wolf

    Most predators have excellent depth perception. Forward-facing eyes form independent images that the brain combines to determine the distance of prey.

    • Species

      Gray wolf Canis lupus

    • Collection Info

      Cast skull

  • E43

    Sea lion impostor: wild boar

    The impressive tusks of this male boar are actually canine teeth that never stop growing. The upper and lower tusks slide against one another, keeping them razor sharp.

    • Species

      Wild boar Sus scrofa

    • Collection Info

      CAS No. 30140

    • Species

  • The Skull Collector

    Ray “Bones” Bandar
    California Academy of Sciences Field Associate

    Ray “Bones” Bandar collected, cleaned, and prepared all of the sea lion skulls you see on the wall above you.

    Employed by the Academy for a few years in the 1950s, he volunteered as a field associate for more than 60 years. Bandar collected more than 6,000 skulls, including roughly 2,000 from California sea lions, building the world’s largest museum collection of this species. His work lives on through its enormous value to the Academy and marine mammal research internationally.

  • Why Collect So Many Skulls?

    The wall above holds more than 400 California sea lion (Zalophus californianus) skulls, a fraction of the Academy’s collection.

    Large collections show similarities and differences between animals, allowing scientists to learn about populations, ecosystems, and animal health. The more specimens we have, the more accurate our data.

    About 75% of the skulls on the wall are adult males. The remaining skulls are from the smaller females or juvenile males (with many positioned toward the center of the wall).

  • Playful Protected Pinnipeds

    California sea lions (Zalophus californianus) are found from Mexico to Canada in onshore groups of several hundred. These smart, playful, and social animals are thriving under the protection of the 1972 Marine Mammal Protection Act.

  • Marine Mammal Stranding Network

    The California Academy of Sciences participates in the Marine Mammal Stranding Network, established by the National Marine Fisheries Service. The Academy and trained volunteers respond to dead marine animals along roughly 165 miles of California coastline. If a dead animal washes ashore, Academy scientists collect bones, DNA, and other valuable information for research purposes.

    Right: Moe Flannery, Academy collections manager, responds to marine mammal strandings along the California coastline from Sonoma County to San Mateo County.
